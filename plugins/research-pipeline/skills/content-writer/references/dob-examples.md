# Sorkin DOB Transformation Examples

Before/after examples showing how to transform research prose into Sorkin DOB narrative
with MoxyWolf voice and natural citation integration.

## Example 1: Opening Hook

### Before (Research Format)
```
For years, CMMC was treated like a big, painful exam:
- Contractors crammed before test day with policies, screenshots, and hastily
  written POA&Ms, hoping a C3PAO would accept narrative over evidence.[1]
- STIGs lived mostly in the ops and engineering corners—seen as "IT hardening,"
  not the primary language of auditors.[2]
```

### After (DOB + MoxyWolf Voice)
```
For years, CMMC worked like every other compliance exam. Cram before test day.
Produce policies and screenshots. Write POA&Ms that sound convincing enough.
Hope the C3PAO accepts your narrative over hard evidence (Fortra, 2026).

It was a storytelling game. If you could talk the talk and generate enough PDFs,
you'd probably pass.

STIGs? Those lived in the ops corner (Steel Patriot Partners, 2024). IT hardening
stuff. Not the language of auditors.

The bargain was implicit but understood: demonstrate intent, not implementation.

That bargain just broke.
```

**Techniques applied:**
- Contractions throughout
- Fragments for emphasis ("IT hardening stuff." / "That bargain just broke.")
- Second-person perspective
- Citations woven naturally: (Fortra, 2026) and (Steel Patriot Partners, 2024)
- Tension builds to a four-word break point

## Example 2: Making Stakes Visceral

### Before (Abstract)
```
The number of contractors needing Level 2, combined with limited assessor
capacity, makes manual evidence generation unsustainable.[5][11]
```

### After (Concrete + Visceral)
```
The numbers make the old approach impossible (Accorian, 2024).

220,000 contractors in the Defense Industrial Base need Level 2 certification.
Maybe 200 certified assessors at full capacity. All racing toward contract
clauses throughout 2026.

Manual evidence collection doesn't scale to those numbers. It can't. You'd need
every assessor working around the clock, and even then the queue backs up for months.

Fortra (2026) isn't being subtle about it: automation is "the only scalable path"
to CMMC Level 2.
```

**Techniques applied:**
- Specific numbers replace vague "limited capacity" (220K vs 200)
- Fragment for impact ("It can't.")
- Reader can do the math themselves
- Timeline pressure ("throughout 2026")
- Citation integrated as narrative authority, not footnote

## Example 3: Unresolved Ending

### Before (Neat Resolution)
```
Organizations that adopt automation will succeed in the new compliance landscape.
```

### After (Sorkin Ending)
```
The question isn't whether automation wins. The question is whether your
organization figures that out before your competitor does – and before DoD
makes the decision for you.

Because they will make it for you. Every contract clause, every tightened
assessment requirement, every vendor that builds STIG automation into their
core offering – they're all voting.

You can participate in that shift. Or you can explain to your contracts team
why you're still doing gap assessments by hand while your competitors are
shipping continuous evidence streams.

Which story do you want to tell?
```

**Techniques applied:**
- No resolution — reader left with a decision
- Competitive pressure ("before your competitor does")
- Concrete consequence ("explain to your contracts team")
- Final question hangs in the air
- Second-person throughout

## Example 4: Voice Injection Integration

### Before (Competent but Generic)
```
STIG compliance automation reduces the labor burden on defense contractors
pursuing CMMC certification.
```

### After (Voice-Injected + DOB)
```
I was working with an organization recently – I can't name them, you just
don't name folks who are failing CMMC audits – and I asked them to walk me
through where they were stuck.

Their staff was spending an inordinate amount of time understanding which
STIGs applied. Then figuring out how to apply them. Reading. Cross-referencing.
Building internal documentation that would be outdated in 90 days.

I told them straight: you don't have a technical problem. You have a staffing
problem. Your people are spending their time reading and understanding when
they should be spending their time deciding.

The reading and understanding part? That should be handled by tools.
```

**Techniques applied:**
- Real story from voice injection interview (Question 2: Evidence)
- First-person authority ("I told them straight")
- The contrarian reframe ("not a technical problem — a staffing problem")
- Specific details without naming the org
- Natural rhythm, varied sentence length
- The insight lands through narrative, not assertion
