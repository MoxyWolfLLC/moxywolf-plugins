# Voice Interview Framework

Detailed guidance for conducting the 8-question voice extraction interview.

## Interview Principles

### One at a time
Never ask two questions in the same message. Each question builds on the previous answer. Batching defeats the purpose — you need to hear how they answered Q1 before you know how to frame Q2.

### Follow the energy
If an answer is thin ("I just fell into it"), probe: "Okay, but what made you STAY?" If an answer is rich, note the patterns — that's where their voice lives.

### Capture exact language
When the author uses a vivid phrase, a specific metaphor, or an unusual construction — note it. Those are voice markers. They go into the "Sounds Like Me" section of the profile.

### Don't interpret too early
During the interview, just collect. Analysis comes after all 8 answers are in. Premature interpretation biases later questions.

## Question-by-Question Guidance

### Q1: Origin Story
**Purpose**: Find the emotional root of their authority.

**If they give a resume answer** ("I've been in compliance for 15 years"):
Redirect: "That's the timeline. I'm looking for the moment — the conversation, the project, the failure — where you thought 'this is what I'm supposed to be doing.'"

**If they struggle**:
Offer an alternative: "Okay, different angle — what's the project or client where you first felt like you really knew what you were doing?"

**What to listen for**: Whether they lead with credentials or stories. Whether the origin is positive (I discovered something) or negative (I saw something broken). Both are valuable voice markers.

### Q2: The Pattern
**Purpose**: Find their intellectual signature.

**If the answer is generic** ("I see that most companies don't take security seriously"):
Push for specificity: "What SPECIFICALLY do you see that tells you they don't? What's the signal you've learned to read?"

**What to listen for**: Systems thinking vs. narrative thinking vs. data thinking. How they connect dots. Whether they use industry jargon naturally or avoid it.

### Q3: How You Argue
**Purpose**: Map their rhetorical instincts.

**If they're unsure** ("I don't know, I just explain my position"):
Give concrete options: "Let me make it specific. You're in a meeting and someone proposes an approach you think is wrong. Do you: (a) tell a story about a time that approach failed, (b) pull up data showing it doesn't work, (c) ask questions until they see the problem themselves, or (d) directly challenge it and explain why it's wrong?"

**What to listen for**: Their default persuasion mode. Whether they escalate or de-escalate. This directly maps to how their prose should flow.

### Q4: What You Hate
**Purpose**: Find where conviction becomes heat.

**If they're polite about it** ("I think the industry could do better with..."):
Give permission: "I'm not looking for diplomatic. What genuinely pisses you off? The thing you've seen a hundred times and still can't believe people do?"

**What to listen for**: The shift in energy. The specific examples that come without prompting. The language gets more natural here — that's voice data.

### Q5: How You Explain
**Purpose**: Capture their teaching style.

**If they pick something too simple**:
Push up: "That was clean. Now pick something harder — something that takes most people in your field 20 minutes to explain. Show me your shortcut."

**What to listen for**: Metaphor preferences. Whether they use analogies from outside their field. How they handle the tension between accuracy and accessibility. Their tolerance for simplification.

### Q6: Your Reader
**Purpose**: Find who they're really writing for.

**If they give a demographic** ("CISOs at mid-market companies"):
Make it a person: "Give me one. What's their name? What happened to them last Tuesday that made your work relevant?"

**What to listen for**: Whether they write for peers, for people below them, for people above them. Whether the reader they picture is in crisis or in growth mode. This shapes tone.

### Q7: The Line You Won't Cross
**Purpose**: Map intellectual boundaries.

**If they can't think of one**:
Try the reverse: "What's advice you see other people in your space give that you think is wrong or at least incomplete?"

**What to listen for**: Where they hold contrarian positions. What they're willing to argue about vs. what they'll just avoid. These boundaries define the edges of their voice.

### Q8: Your Natural Register
**Purpose**: Calibrate the gap between their spoken and written voice.

**If they say "I write like I talk"**:
Test it: "Read back your answer to Q4 — the thing that makes you angry. Would you write it exactly that way, or would you clean it up?"

**If they name a reference** ("I write like [author]"):
Note the reference but don't over-index. Ask what SPECIFICALLY about that author's style they relate to — sentence length? Tone? Structure? Humor?

**What to listen for**: Self-awareness about formality. Whether they consciously shift registers or not. This determines how much "cleanup" to do between their raw Q&A answers and generated prose.

## Post-Interview Analysis Checklist

After all 8 answers are collected, analyze:

- [ ] **Argument mode identified** (deductive/narrative/Socratic/confrontational/hybrid)
- [ ] **Sentence rhythm observed** from their actual answers (not assumed)
- [ ] **Vocabulary register mapped** (technical depth, jargon comfort)
- [ ] **Emotional triggers cataloged** (what heats them up, what cools them down)
- [ ] **Teaching style captured** (metaphor type, simplification approach)
- [ ] **Forbidden patterns listed** (things they'd never write)
- [ ] **"Sounds Like Me" passages extracted** (3-5 from their actual interview answers)
- [ ] **Anti-detection markers calibrated** to their specific style
- [ ] **Self-reported register cross-checked** against actual interview language
