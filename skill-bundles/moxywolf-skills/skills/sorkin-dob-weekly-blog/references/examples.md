# Transformation Examples

Before/after examples showing how to transform research prose into Sorkin DOB narrative with MoxyWolf voice.

## Example 1: Opening Hook Transformation

### Before (Research Format)

```
## Act I – The audit that outgrew the checklist  

For years, CMMC was treated like a big, painful exam:  
- Contractors crammed before test day with policies, screenshots, and hastily written POA&Ms, hoping a C3PAO would accept narrative over evidence.[1]
- STIGs lived mostly in the ops and engineering corners—seen as "IT hardening," not the primary language of auditors.[2]

The implicit bargain was: if you can talk a good game and produce enough PDFs, you might pass.
```

### After (Published Format with Citations)

```
For years, CMMC worked like every other compliance exam. Cram before test day. Produce policies and screenshots. Write POA&Ms that sound convincing enough. Hope the C3PAO accepts your narrative over hard evidence (Fortra, 2026).

It was a storytelling game. If you could talk the talk and generate enough PDFs, you'd probably pass.

STIGs? Those lived in the ops corner (Steel Patriot Partners, 2024). IT hardening stuff. Not the language of auditors. Not what wins or loses certifications.

The bargain was implicit but understood: demonstrate intent, not implementation. Promise capability, don't prove it.

That bargain just broke.
```

**What Changed:**
- Contractions: "It was," "you'd," "don't"
- Fragments for emphasis: "IT hardening stuff," "That bargain just broke"
- Varying sentence length (4 words to 40+ words)
- Second-person perspective puts reader in experience
- In-text citations integrated naturally: (Fortra, 2026) and (Steel Patriot Partners, 2024)
- Tension builds to break point

## Example 2: Embedding Citations Organically

### Before (Research Format with Brackets)

```
New guidance and vendor content describe STIGs and CIS benchmarks as shared implementation rails that can simultaneously satisfy DoD expectations and feed CMMC evidence.[8][2]

Automation platforms start promising "STIG in, CMMC out": harden systems at scale, continuously scan them, and emit auditor-ready mappings and reports.[9][10]
```

### After (Natural Attribution with Citations)

```
Steel Patriot Partners (2024) and Cimcor (2024) both make versions of the same argument: properly implemented STIGs and CIS benchmarks don't just satisfy DoD security expectations. They become your evidence stream for CMMC assessments.

SteelCloud (2024) is betting their product roadmap on a single pitch: "STIG in, CMMC out." Harden your systems using baseline configurations, scan them continuously, and let the automation generate the audit evidence you need.
```

**What Changed:**
- Bracketed citations [8][2][9][10] replaced with (Organization, Year) format
- Source authority woven into sentence structure
- Direct vendor positioning adds credibility
- Citations integrated naturally without interrupting flow
- Reader understands who's making claims and when

## Example 3: Visceral Stakes with Citations

### Before (Abstract)

```
As 2026 opens, the plot tension is scale:  
- The number of contractors needing Level 2, combined with limited assessor capacity, makes manual evidence generation and one-off gap assessments obviously unsustainable.[5][11]
```

### After (Concrete with Citations)

```
The numbers make the old approach impossible (Accorian, 2024).

220,000 contractors in the Defense Industrial Base need Level 2 certification. Maybe 200 certified assessors at full capacity. All racing toward contract clauses throughout 2026.

Manual evidence collection doesn't scale to those numbers. It can't. You'd need every assessor working around the clock, and even then the queue backs up for months. One-off gap assessments? They're artifacts of a system that assumed scarcity, not ubiquity.

Fortra (2026) isn't being subtle about it: automation is "the only scalable path" to CMMC Level 2.
```

**What Changed:**
- Specific numbers replace vague "limited capacity"
- Reader can do the math themselves (220K vs 200)
- Second-person perspective ("you'd need")
- Fragments for impact ("It can't")
- Concrete timeline pressure ("throughout 2026")
- Visual language ("queue backs up")
- In-text citations integrated: (Accorian, 2024) and Fortra (2026)

## Example 4: Unresolved Dramatic Ending

### Before (Neat Resolution)

```
In other words, the narrative is evolving from "How do we pass this audit?" to "How do we live in a world where audits are just snapshots of a continuously proven, STIG-anchored security posture?"
```

### After (Dramatic Tension)

```
The question isn't whether automation wins. The question is whether your organization figures that out before your competitor does – and before DoD makes the decision for you.

Because they will make it for you. Every contract clause, every tightened assessment requirement, every vendor that builds STIG automation into their core offering – they're all voting. And the result is already visible.

You can participate in that shift. Or you can explain to your contracts team why you're still doing gap assessments by hand while your competitors are shipping continuous evidence streams.

Which story do you want to tell?
```

**What Changed:**
- From philosophical question to immediate choice
- Stakes made personal and urgent ("your organization," "your competitor")
- Future consequence made concrete ("explain to your contracts team")
- No neat resolution – reader left with decision to make
- Final question hangs in the air
